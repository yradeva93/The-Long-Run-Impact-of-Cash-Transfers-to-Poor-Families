A. Aizer, S. Eli, J. Ferrie, and A. Lleras-Muney estimate the long-run impact of unconditional cash transfers on children's longevity, income, health and educational attainment. They used individual-level data of the Mothers' Pension program and matched it to Census, WWII  data, and DMF (Death Master File) records. The results suggest that compared to children of rejected mothers, an accepted male child lived one year longer, obtained one-third more years of schooling, was less likely to be underweight, and had higher income.
